01The one-line positioning

Describe an app to your friends and it exists — on your devices, not our servers.

Supporting lines, by audience:

Privacy / dev audience

"The server only moves encrypted blobs. We couldn't read your data if we wanted to."

Mainstream audience

"Group apps without the app. One sentence, one QR code, no signup."

Build-in-public audience

"A multiplayer app platform that costs almost nothing to run, because your devices are the database."

What we are not positioned as A better group chat, a Notion competitor, or a no-code builder. Those framings invite feature-checklist comparisons we lose. The frame we own is conjuring + sovereignty + calm — incumbents structurally can't copy "we can't read you and we won't notify you."

02The demo is the marketing

The core asset is a ~20-second screen recording. Everything else — threads, landing pages, docs — exists to make people watch it and believe it.

type: "a place where my 4 roommates vote on what to cook this week" 3s: the app appears, working tap: Share → QR fills the screen phone: friend scans with the camera → they're in, no signup both: editing live
The hard consequence No public launch until in-app generation ships. The current copy-paste-to-ChatGPT flow is a fine power-user path, but it kills the demo dead. Friends-and-family rollout of the existing templates can and should happen before that — it battle-tests the relay and produces real usage anecdotes for the build-in-public threads.

03Audiences — three rings, in order

Ring 1 · Local-first / privacy / indie-dev community

  • Where: the X local-first scene, Hacker News (Show HN), lobste.rs, r/selfhosted, local-first Discords and meetups.
  • Why first: they amplify, they stress-test the architecture, BYOK / self-host-the-relay is built for them, and their endorsement is the trust signal Ring 3 needs later.
  • How: publish the architecture and threat model openly. For this audience the honest threat model — what the relay operator can see (hashed room ids, IPs, timing, blob sizes) and can't (any content) — is more persuasive than any copy. Self-hosting the relay is one Go binary; say so loudly.

Ring 2 · Build-in-public / indie hackers (X)

  • Why: Gil's natural channel; the story of a solo operator running a multiplayer platform for a few dollars a month is itself content.
  • Thread material already in hand: the dumb-relay design; the cost math (relay-as-cache, devices as backup); "your data outlives our servers and dies when you all let it go"; the $0.0006 generation; ambient AI where your friends' devices are the cloud.
  • Cadence: build-in-public threads during the run-up → demo video at launch → Show HN once one-tap generation works → Product Hunt after the rough edges are gone.

Ring 3 · End users, via use-cases — never via "the platform"

  • Nobody searches for "rooms"; they search for split trip expenses, chore chart for roommates, book club tracker. Each template gets its own landing page / keyword on the website, all feeding the same PWA. The website How-to-guides backlog item slots here.
  • Trip Split is the sharpest wedge: every trip group contains non-users, the link-share moment is built into the use-case, and the value is immediate and numeric.
  • The growth loop (make room → share link → friend joins with no signup → friend makes their own room) does the rest; our job is only the first room.

04Launch gates — technical work that must precede the public moment

Ordered. The first two are hard gates; the rest are strongly preferred. All four are now scored rows in the RICE backlog.

  1. Stateless generation proxy + in-app "speak it into existence" — the demo depends on it (business plan §09, next-steps #2–3).
  2. Relay lazy-load + idle eviction, plus abuse caps — per-IP rate limits, max blob size, max rooms per source. A viral spike with all-rooms-in-RAM and no caps either falls over or gets abused as free encrypted storage. This must land before the demo can go viral, not after.
  3. Signed-tombstone room delete — "the right to destroy" is a visible feature and a Ring 1 talking point.
  4. Threat model, published (HTML) — formalize the business plan's "honest limitations" into adversary profiles: relay operator, network observer, ex-member with the old code, device thief — what each can and can't do, with accepted limitations stated plainly (no re-key on member removal; metadata visible; generation prompts leave the device unless BYOK).
  5. Onboarding & PWA install polish — already backlog #2; the QR scan must land somewhere that doesn't feel rough.

05Messaging discipline (load-bearing copy rules)

Never blur the privacy line

"Room content is E2E encrypted and never readable by us" and "your one-sentence app description goes to our AI proxy" are both true; conflating them in either direction loses the trust moat. Generation touches app descriptions, never room content.

Say the limitations out loud, unprompted

Metadata visibility, room-code-is-the-key, no public-channel re-key. Ring 1 will find them anyway; finding them in our docs builds trust, finding them on their own builds a takedown thread.

Market moments, not the platform

Lead with one use-case per asset. "Rooms can do anything" is a weaker message than "watch me make a potluck app in one sentence."

06Open questions (to decide, not yet decided)

  • Name/scope of the public story: launch as "Rooms by The Idea Guy", or lead with a single template's brand (e.g. Trip Split) and reveal the platform second?
  • Pricing timing: launch Free + BYOK only and add Rooms Plus once the ambient helper exists, or announce all three lanes day one? → analyzed in Pricing & Operating Costs.
  • Geo/latency: a single relay region is fine pre-PMF; decide the threshold that triggers sharding work.
